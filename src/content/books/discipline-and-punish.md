## My reading

This book should be required reading for anyone working in security. Not because Foucault is pro-surveillance — the opposite. *Discipline and Punish* traces how surveillance transforms from a technology of punishment into the background condition of modern social life.

The Panopticon chapter is the centerpiece: Bentham's prison design where inmates never know if they're being watched, so they internalize the guard. Foucault's argument is that this design escaped the prison and became the template for schools, hospitals, factories, and — he was writing in 1975, before the internet — eventually everywhere.

## The security professional's reading

When I'm building security monitoring systems, I'm building Panopticons. Logs, SIEM alerts, endpoint monitoring — the entire threat detection stack is architecturally identical to what Foucault describes.

This doesn't make it wrong. Foucault is describing a mechanism, not condemning it. But it means I should think carefully about:

- Who is being watched and why
- Who has access to the surveillance output
- What behaviors the act of observation itself changes

> The power of the Norm appears through the disciplines. Is this the birth of a new kind of individuality?

## Where I push back

Foucault's analysis is so complete that it accidentally produces paralysis. If surveillance is constitutive of modern subjectivity, what's outside it? His work is better as a diagnostic than a prescription.

## The intersection

Every access control system embeds a theory of trust. Every logging decision is a claim about what matters. Reading Foucault made me realize that security architecture is not neutral — it encodes assumptions about people, risk, and power that are worth examining explicitly.
