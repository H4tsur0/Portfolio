## My reading

Nietzsche is the hardest philosopher to read honestly, because he's also the easiest to misread romantically. *Beyond Good and Evil* is his attack on the complacency of moral certainty — the idea that "good" and "evil" are fixed categories handed down from somewhere above.

What struck me most was the concept of the *philosopher of the future* — someone who doesn't just accept inherited frameworks but creates new values. Reading this while studying cybersecurity felt strange at first, then clarifying. Security work is fundamentally about understanding systems well enough to see what they assume. Nietzsche was doing the same thing with moral philosophy.

> The noble type of man regards *himself* as a determiner of values; he does not require to be approved of; he passes the judgement: "What is injurious to me is injurious in itself."

## Questions this raised

- Where does the line sit between *questioning assumptions* (healthy skepticism) and *rationalized nihilism* (using critique as permission to act without ethics)?
- Nietzsche's contempt for "herd morality" is compelling but dangerous — it's been historically abused. How do you hold the insight without the toxicity?

## The cybersecurity intersection

Penetration testing requires adopting an adversarial mindset — thinking like someone who doesn't share your ethical constraints. Nietzsche's framework helped me think about what that means more carefully. The skill is not the same as the disposition.
