## My reading

Pretty fun book to read especially the fact that the main personnel also has the same name i do. overall the book was not only about philosophy, it was also about wealth and how people missunderstood wealth.

## Flow

Flow isn't about being lazy; it’s about efficiency. Just as water naturally finds the easiest way down a mountain, wealth follows the path where value meets the least friction. In the book, Richard learns that everyone has a "natural frequency." When you work within that frequency, work feels like play, and results come exponentially faster.

> "Don’t follow the tracks of others; follow the tracks that you make yourself."

## Wink

It’s easy to get caught in the "grind" of a side hustle or the heavy technicalities of a career, but the lesson of the Woodcutter is a stark reminder that raw labor is a trap without leverage. It makes me realize that the value I’m building isn’t just in the certificates or the hours spent at a desk, but in the internal vision—the "wink"—that allows me to see systems and opportunities where others just see noise.

> "To see what others do not see, you must look where others do not look."

## Effort

I’ve realized that I used to mistake 'being busy' for 'being productive', now i see that raw effort is alike the 24 drops of water and if I spend all of it just 'carrying the bucket' for today’s results, I’ll never have the energy to build the 'pipeline' for tomorrow. It has pushed me to stop glorifying the struggle and start looking for the leverage point in everything I do. Now, instead of just swinging a blunt axe with more force, I’m prioritizing the time to sharpen my tools and sharpen my vision, ensuring that every bit of energy I use is an investment in a system rather than just a trade for a paycheck.

> "The harder you work, the less you earn. The more you learn, the more you yearn. The more you give, the more you live."

## Questions this raised

- What is my 'Path of Least Resistance'?
- What am I seeing that everyone else is just looking at?
